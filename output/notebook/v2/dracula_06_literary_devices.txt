
Chapter 1:
- Personification: "The baying of the wolves sounded nearer and nearer, as though they were closing round on us from every side." The wolves' howling is described as if the wolves themselves are actively surrounding the carriage.

- Imagery: "Sometimes the hills were so steep that, despite our driver’s haste, the horses could only go slowly. I wished to get down and walk up them, as we do at home, but the driver would not hear of it." Vivid visual imagery helps the reader picture the steep, winding roads.

- Foreshadowing: "For myself, I felt a sort of paralysis of fear. It is only when a man feels himself face to face with such horrors that he can understand their true import." This hints that even worse horrors are still to come. 

- Symbolism: The howling wolves represent the supernatural dangers and evil forces surrounding the carriage on its journey to Dracula's castle.


Chapter 2:
- Foreshadowing: The description of Dracula's home as a "veritable prison" foreshadows that Jonathan will be trapped there. 

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "a stone falling from the window would fall a thousand feet without touching anything!" create strong mental images.

- Symbolism: The crucifix represents faith which protects Jonathan from Dracula's fury.

- Personification: Describing the sunrise as having "preternatural shrillness" gives it human-like qualities.


Chapter 3:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes seeing Dracula crawling down the castle wall, this foreshadows Dracula's supernatural abilities and hints at the dangers to come. 

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe the castle setting, like "great jagged mountain fastnesses, rising peak on peak." This helps create an ominous, Gothic atmosphere.

- Symbolism: The crucifix Jonathan places over his bed symbolizes the struggle between good and evil forces.

- Characterization: Dracula's dialogue reveals his pride in his warrior ancestry and establishes his authoritative, commanding personality.


Chapter 4:
- Foreshadowing: "I must watch for proof. Of one thing I am glad: if it was that the Count carried me here and undressed me, he must have been hurried in his task, for my pockets are intact." This foreshadows that the Count will attempt to go through Harker's belongings later.

- Imagery: "With hands that trembled with eagerness, I unhooked the chains and drew back the massive bolts." This creates a vivid image of Harker eagerly trying to escape.

- Symbolism: The castle represents danger and entrapment. Harker is trapped inside, at the mercy of Dracula.

- Metaphor: "But still the door continued slowly to open, and only the Count’s body stood in the gap. Suddenly it struck me that this might be the moment and means of my doom." The door opening with only Dracula in the gap is a metaphor for Dracula controlling Harker's fate.


Chapter 5:
- Personification: "I feel sorry, really and truly sorry, for two of the poor fellows." This personifies the "poor fellows" by describing them with human emotions like sorrow.  

- Hyperbole: "Some girls are so vain! You and I, Mina dear, who are engaged and are going to settle down soon soberly into old married women, can despise vanity." Calling themselves "old married women" exaggerates how seriously Lucy views her own impending marriage.

- Imagery: "He put it very nicely, saying that he did not want to wring my confidence from me, but only to know, because if a woman’s heart was free a man might have hope." This creates a vivid image of wringing water (confidence) from a sponge (her heart).

- Foreshadowing: "I can fancy what a wonderful power he must have over his patients. He has a curious habit of looking one straight in the face, as if trying to read one’s thoughts." This description of Dr. Seward foreshadows his role using hypnotism later in the story.


Chapter 6:
- Foreshadowing: "Some day soon the Angel of Death will sound his trumpet for me." This foreshadows Mr. Swales' death.

- Imagery: "Dark figures are on the beach here and there, sometimes half shrouded in the mist, and seem “men like trees walking.”" This imagery helps the reader visualize the scene.

- Personification: "The sea is tumbling in over the shallows and the sandy flats with a roar, muffled in the sea-mists drifting inland." The sea is described as if it were a living thing. 

- Symbolism: The strange ship likely symbolizes impending danger or trouble. Its erratic movements reflect the tumultuous events to come.


Chapter 7:
- Foreshadowing: The description of the large, strange dog foreshadows its later appearance on the ship, showing something sinister is afoot.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "the sea, running mountains high, threw skywards with each wave mighty masses of white foam" create strong visual imagery.

- Personification: The sea is personified as a "roaring and devouring monster" to emphasize its dangerous power.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and beads tied to the ship's wheel symbolize the captain's desperate religious faith in his final hours.


Chapter 8:
- Foreshadowing: Mina writes "If Mr. Holmwood fell in love with her seeing her only in the drawing-room, I wonder what he would say if he saw her now." This foreshadows Lucy's later vampiric transformation.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the abbey ruins create vivid mental images, like "the silver light of the moon struck a half-reclining figure, snowy white." 

- Symbolism: The moonlight and shadows seem to symbolize the supernatural forces at work.

- Personification: Renfield refers to Dracula as his "Master" and begs for his commands, personifying Dracula as a godlike figure.

- Dramatic irony: The reader knows Lucy is being attacked by Dracula at night, while the characters remain unaware of the cause of her illness.


Chapter 9:
- Foreshadowing: Mina's letter to Lucy foreshadows future events, hinting that Jonathan has suffered some terrible shock related to his illness. This builds suspense and anticipation for the reader.

- Imagery: Descriptions of sunsets and the asylum create vivid mental images, allowing the reader to visualize the scenes.

- Symbolism: The flies that Renfield catches represent his madness and obsession. When he discards them, it symbolizes his temporary return to sanity.

- Dramatic irony: The characters are unaware of the supernatural forces at play, while the reader has more knowledge, creating irony and tension.

- Personification: The sunset is described as having "lurid lights and inky shadows," giving human traits to the natural landscape.


Chapter 10:
- Personification: "Dame Nature gathers round a foreign body an envelope of some insensitive tissue which can protect from evil that which it would otherwise harm by contact." Nature is personified as actively protecting Lucy.

- Imagery: "Her breathing was painful to see or hear." This visual and auditory imagery helps the reader imagine Lucy's weakened state.

- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing's wreath of garlic flowers foreshadows his belief that Lucy is threatened by an evil force. 

- Symbolism: The wreath of garlic symbolizes Van Helsing's intent to protect Lucy from harm.


Chapter 11:
- Foreshadowing: Lucy writes "I feel I am dying of weakness, and have barely strength to write, but it must be done if I die in the doing." This foreshadows her later death.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the pillar of dust that travellers describe when there is a simoon in the desert" create vivid imagery.

- Symbolism: The wreath of flowers that Lucy tears off represents Dr. Van Helsing's protections being removed.

- Personification: Referring to the "passing bell" tolling and the dogs howling personifies the sounds.


Chapter 12:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "It is not yet too late! Quick! quick! Bring the brandy!" This foreshadows that they will try to revive Lucy.

- Imagery: Descriptions of Lucy's physical appearance create vivid imagery, like "Her breathing grew stertorous, the mouth opened, and the pale gums, drawn back, made the teeth look longer and sharper than they had been in the morning." 

- Symbolism: The garlic flowers represent trying to ward off evil forces afflicting Lucy. When she tears them off her neck, it symbolizes those forces overpowering her.

- Personification: "The wings of the angel of death" personifies death.

- Tone: The overall ominous, tense tone reflects the uncertainty around whether Lucy will survive.


Chapter 13:
- Personification: "Loneliness will sit over our roofs with brooding wings." This gives loneliness human-like qualities.

- Imagery: "Where the air is fresh, and the sun rises over Hampstead Hill, and where wild flowers grow of their own accord." This creates a vivid image in the reader's mind.

- Foreshadowing: "There will be pain for us all; but it will not be all pain, nor will this pain be the last." This hints at future events. 

- Symbolism: The "bloofer lady" seems to symbolize some dark force luring the children away.


Chapter 14:
- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing says "There are darknesses in life, and there are lights; you are one of the lights. You will have happy life and good life, and your husband will be blessed in you." This foreshadows that dark events will happen later in the story.

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe Van Helsing, like "The poise of the head strikes one at once as indicative of thought and power; the head is noble, well-sized, broad, and large behind the ears." This helps the reader visualize the character.

- Symbolism: Lucy's mysterious illness and puncture wounds on her neck symbolize her transformation into a vampire.

- Dramatic irony: The characters don't realize yet that Lucy has become a vampire, but the reader knows based on the hints provided.


Chapter 15:
- Foreshadowing: "If it be so, farewell." Van Helsing's note left in his portmanteau foreshadows upcoming tragic events.

- Imagery: "The sky was overcast, and somewhere far off an early cock crew." Descriptive language paints a vivid picture.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and garlic seal up Lucy's tomb, symbolizing tools used against evil. 

- Personification: "But the Professor never stopped for a moment; he sawed down a couple of feet along one side of the lead coffin, and then across, and down the other side." The coffin is given lifelike attributes.


Chapter 16:
- Imagery: "The night was dark with occasional gleams of moonlight between the rents of the heavy clouds that scudded across the sky." This description creates a vivid image in the reader's mind of the setting.

- Foreshadowing: "When she saw us she drew back with an angry snarl, such as a cat gives when taken unawares." This foreshadows Lucy's later aggressive and animalistic behavior. 

- Personification: "There was gladness and mirth and peace everywhere, for we were at rest ourselves on one account." Gladness, mirth, and peace are given human attributes.

- Symbolism: The stake and hammer used to kill Lucy symbolize the tools needed to defeat evil.


Chapter 17:
- Foreshadowing: When Mina says "If ever the future should bring to you a time when you need a man's help, believe me, you will not call in vain." This foreshadows that Mina and Arthur may need each other's help against Dracula in the future. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the tears rained down his cheeks" and "shook with emotion" create vivid images of Arthur's grief.

- Symbolism: Lucy is repeatedly referred to as "poor dear Lucy", symbolizing her innocence and tragic fate. 

- Metaphor: Mina comforting Arthur is compared to a mother comforting a child, representing feminine compassion.

- Personification: Renfield's sanity is personified when he is described as "placidly sitting" and "smiling benignly."


Chapter 18:
- Personification: "When we had finished, Mrs. Harker said:— 'Dr. Seward, may I ask a favour? I want to see your patient, Mr. Renfield.'" This personifies Renfield by referring to him as if he is a normal patient rather than a lunatic.

- Foreshadowing: "When I went to the station to meet Van Helsing I left the boys behind me. Poor Art seemed more cheerful than he has been since Lucy first took ill, and Quincey is more like his own bright self than he has been for many a long day." This foreshadows coming events related to Lucy's illness.

- Imagery: "He can transform himself to wolf, as we gather from the ship arrival in Whitby, when he tear open the dog; he can be as bat, as Madam Mina saw him on the window at Whitby, and as friend John saw him fly from this so near house, and as my friend Quincey saw him at the window of Miss Lucy." This imagery helps the reader visualize Dracula's abilities.

- Symbolism: "The Draculas were, says Arminius, a great and noble race, though now and again were scions who were held by their coevals to have had dealings with the Evil One. They learned his secrets in the Scholomance, amongst the mountains over Lake Hermanstadt, where the devil claims the tenth scholar as his due." The Scholomance symbolizes Dracula's association with evil.
- Personification: Referring to Renfield as a normal patient rather than a lunatic personifies him.

- Foreshadowing: Lucy's worsening illness foreshadows coming events related to her condition.  

- Imagery: Descriptions of Dracula's abilities as a wolf, bat etc. create vivid imagery for the reader.

- Symbolism: The Scholomance represents Dracula's connection to evil forces.


Chapter 19:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes seeing Count Dracula's face in the shadows, it foreshadows their later direct encounters with the Count. 

- Imagery: Detailed descriptions of the sights, sounds, and smells in Dracula's castle create vivid imagery for the reader. For example, the "fluffy and heavy" dust and "masses of spider's webs".

- Symbolism: The crucifix given to Jonathan symbolizes the battle against evil forces.

- Personification: Mist creeping towards the house is described as having a "sentience and vitality of its own", personifying the mist.


Chapter 20:
- Foreshadowing: "If we could only get some hint as to what passed in his mind, between the time of my argument with him to-day and his resumption of fly-catching, it might afford us a valuable clue." This foreshadows that something important is going to happen with Renfield soon.

- Imagery: "His moods have so followed the doings of the Count, that the coming destruction of the monster may be carried to him in some subtle way." This imagery describes how Renfield's moods are connected to the Count. 

- Symbolism: The flies symbolize Renfield's madness and obsession. His catching flies represents his unstable mental state.

- Dramatic irony: The reader knows Renfield heard the "wild yell" from the Count, but the characters don't realize this yet. This creates dramatic irony.


Chapter 21:
- Personification: "His eyes flamed red with devilish passion". This describes Dracula's eyes as if they have human emotions.

- Imagery: "The moonlight was so bright that through the thick yellow blind the room was light enough to see". This creates a vivid visual image of the moonlit room. 

- Foreshadowing: "We must work to-morrow!’ He said the latter words through his shut teeth". This foreshadows impending dramatic events.

- Simile: "With a wrench, which threw his victim back upon the bed as though hurled from a height, he turned and sprang at us". This compares Dracula hurling Mina to something being thrown from a height.

- Metaphor: "Then indeed, my heart sank within me". This metaphorically describes the feeling of despair.


Chapter 22:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing places the Sacred Wafer on Mina's forehead and it burns her, foreshadowing that she may have to bear the mark until Judgment Day, hinting she may become a vampire. 

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "her overwrought nature had its voice in that dreadful scream" and "Pulling her beautiful hair over her face, as the leper of old his mantle, she wailed out".

- Symbolism: The boxes of earth symbolize the Count's connection to his homeland and his undead nature. Sterilizing them represents defeating him.

- Personification: "We sat still; my own cigar burnt furiously, but Van Helsing’s went cold altogether." The cigars are given human attributes of burning furiously or going cold.


Chapter 23:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "He can live for centuries, and you are but mortal woman. Time is now to be dreaded—since once he put that mark upon your throat." This foreshadows that Mina is in grave danger from Dracula because of his immortality. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "a drawn, haggard old man, whose white hair matches well with the hollow burning eyes and grief-written lines of his face." Paint vivid images in the reader's mind.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and wafer represent spiritual weapons against Dracula's evil.

- Personification: "The gladness of our hearts must have shown upon our faces." Gives human attributes to the abstract concept of gladness.


Chapter 24:
- Personification: "This battle is but begun, and in the end we shall win—so sure as that God sits on high to watch over His children." 

This personifies God as sitting on high and watching over people like a parent watches over children.

- Foreshadowing: "There must be no chance lost, and in Varna we must be ready to act the instant when that ship arrives.” 

This foreshadows that an important event will happen when the ship arrives in Varna.

- Imagery: "The same power that compels her silence may compel her speech."

This creates a vivid image of the supernatural power affecting Mina. 

- Symbolism: "We shall all be armed, in all ways; and when the time for the end has come, our effort shall not be lack."

Being armed symbolizes being prepared to fight the evil Count Dracula.


Chapter 25:
- Personification: "My soul is freer than it has been since that awful hour." The soul is described as if it is a person that can be free or trapped.

- Foreshadowing: "He think, too, that as he cut himself off from knowing your mind, there can be no knowledge of him to you; there is where he fail!" This foreshadows that Dracula's belief he has escaped them will prove to be wrong.

- Imagery: "We shall follow him; and we shall not flinch; even if we peril ourselves that we become like him." This evokes a vivid image of determined pursuit. 

- Symbolism: The "baptism of blood" Dracula gave Mina symbolizes her connection to him and the darkness.


Chapter 26:
- Foreshadowing: "If the Count escape us this time—and he is strong and subtle and cunning—he may choose to sleep him for a century, and then in time our dear one would come to him to keep him company." This foreshadows the possibility that if they don't stop Dracula now, he may return in the future to turn Mina into a vampire.

- Imagery: "The morning is bitterly cold; the furnace heat is grateful, though we have heavy fur coats." This description helps the reader imagine and feel the cold morning air contrasted with the warmth of the furnace. 

- Symbolism: The box carrying Dracula is symbolic of Dracula's confinement and the mystery surrounding his plans.

- Personification: "Whilst they are resting, I shall go over all carefully, and perhaps I may arrive at some conclusion. I shall try to follow the Professor’s example, and think without prejudice on the facts before me...." Mina personifies "conclusion" and "facts" as if they are people she can interact with.


Chapter 27:
- Foreshadowing: "I began to fear—horrible fears; but then came to me the sense of safety in that ring wherein I stood." This foreshadows the later scene where Van Helsing uses the ring of holy wafers to protect Mina from the vampire women.

- Imagery: "The snow was now falling more heavily, and swirled about fiercely, for a high wind was beginning to blow." This creates a vivid image of the snowstorm. 

- Symbolism: The box carried by the gypsies represents Dracula himself, as it contains his earthly remains. When Morris and Jonathan destroy the box, it symbolizes Dracula's destruction.

- Personification: "Instinctively they reined in, and at the instant Lord Godalming and Jonathan dashed up at one side and Dr. Seward and Mr. Morris on the other." The horses are personified as acting instinctively.
