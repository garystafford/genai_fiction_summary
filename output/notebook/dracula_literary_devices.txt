
Chapter 1:
- Foreshadowing: The howling of the wolves and dogs foreshadows the later appearance of the wolves surrounding the carriage. The driver's comment about the dogs being "too fierce" also foreshadows this.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the sights and sounds create vivid images, like "the steam from our hard-driven horses rose in a white cloud" and the "endless perspective of jagged rock and pointed crags."

- Symbolism: The crucifix given to the narrator symbolizes the fear of evil forces and things supernatural. Later, the moonlight seems to affect the wolves, symbolizing the supernatural.


Chapter 2:
- Foreshadowing: The descriptions of Dracula's home being like a prison foreshadow that Jonathan will be trapped there.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions are used to create imagery, like "a stone falling from the window would fall a thousand feet without touching anything!" 

- Symbolism: The crucifix represents faith which protects Jonathan from Dracula's fury.

- Personification: The line "the dawn, which is like, in its way, the turn of the tide" personifies dawn and tide.

- Metaphor: Comparing Dracula's teeth to sharp white fangs is a metaphor.

- Mood: An uneasy, ominous mood is created through the gothic setting and events.


Chapter 3:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes seeing Dracula crawling down the castle wall like a lizard, this foreshadows Dracula's inhuman, monstrous nature that is later revealed. 

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe the setting, like "great jagged mountain fastnesses, rising peak on peak, the sheer rock studded with mountain ash and thorn." This helps create an ominous, gothic atmosphere.

- Symbolism: The crucifix Jonathan touches represents safety and comfort in the face of the evil in the castle. It symbolizes the struggle between good and evil.

- Personification: Dracula's eyebrows are personified as "a heaving bar of white-hot metal" which gives them a sinister, inhuman quality.

- Simile: Jonathan compares his situation to being "like a rat in a trap." This simile conveys his helplessnesss and fear.


Chapter 4:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan says "I know now the span of my life. God help me!" This foreshadows that Dracula plans to kill him soon.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the women waiting to "suck my blood" creates a vivid image.

- Symbolism: The gold coins symbolize Dracula's ancient evil.

- Metaphor: Comparing Dracula to a "filthy leech" creates a metaphor emphasizing his monstrous nature.

- Personification: The line "Despair has its own calms" personifies despair as a living entity.


Chapter 5:
- Personification: "I feel sorry, really and truly sorry, for two of the poor fellows." Lucy personifies the proposals as if they are people she feels sympathy for.

- Imagery: "He put it very nicely, saying that he did not want to wring my confidence from me, but only to know, because if a woman’s heart was free a man might have hope." This creates a vivid image of wringing or squeezing confidence out of Lucy.

- Hyperbole: "I know I would if I were free—only I don’t want to be free. My dear, I must stop here at present, I feel so miserable, though I am so happy." Lucy exaggerates her emotions of being both extremely miserable and happy at the same time.

- Allusion: "I sympathise with poor Desdemona when she had such a dangerous stream poured in her ear, even by a black man." Morris alludes to Shakespeare's Othello.

- Foreshadowing: "I presume that the sanguine temperament itself and the disturbing influence end in a mentally-accomplished finish; a possibly dangerous man, probably dangerous if unselfish." Dr. Seward's notes foreshadow Renfield's dangerous tendencies.


Chapter 6:
- Foreshadowing: The descriptions of the storm brewing and the strange ship foreshadow ominous events to come. 

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions of the scenery, like "the valley is beautifully green, and it is so steep that when you are on the high land on either side you look right across it, unless you are near enough to see down."

- Symbolism: The white lady ghost in the abbey ruins symbolizes a tragic past. The abbey itself symbolizes decay.

- Personification: The sea is described as having "fingers" that "stretch like grey fingers" and making a "roar, muffled in the sea-mists."


Chapter 7:
- Foreshadowing: The description of the large, strange dog foreshadows its later appearance on the ship, showing something sinister is afoot. 

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "the sea, running mountains high, threw skywards with each wave mighty masses of white foam" create strong visual imagery.

- Personification: The sea is personified as a "roaring and devouring monster" to emphasize its dangerous power.

- Simile: Comparisons like "as idle as a painted ship upon a painted ocean" are used to help readers visualize the scene.

- Symbolism: The crucifix tied to the ship captain likely symbolizes his faith and conviction in steering the ship despite the horrors aboard.


Chapter 8:
- Foreshadowing: "I must be careful not to let it grow into a habit. No, I shall take none to-night! I have thought of Lucy, and I shall not dishonour her by mixing the two." This foreshadows Dr. Seward's later addiction to chloral.

- Imagery: "The setting sun, low down in the sky, was just dropping behind Kettleness; the red light was thrown over on the East Cliff and the old abbey, and seemed to bathe everything in a beautiful rosy glow." Vivid sensory imagery helps the reader visualize the scene.

- Symbolism: The moonlight and shadows symbolize the interplay between good and evil forces. 

- Personification: "Chasing an errant swarm of bees is nothing to following a naked lunatic, when the fit of escaping is upon him!" Renfield's madness is personified as an entity with agency.

- Allusion: Reference to Jack Sheppard alludes to the notorious 18th century criminal, highlighting Renfield's dangerousness.


Chapter 9:
- Foreshadowing: Mina's letter to Lucy foreshadows future events, hinting that Jonathan has suffered some terrible shock and trauma. This builds suspense and anticipation for the reader.

- Imagery: Descriptions of sunsets and the asylum create vivid images in the reader's mind. This brings the setting to life.

- Symbolism: The flies that Renfield catches represent his madness and obsession. The sunset may symbolize impending darkness and danger.

- Personification: The sunset is described as having "marvelous tints" as if it is a living being. This gives human-like qualities to a non-human subject.

- Allusion: Reference is made to Disraeli, assuming the reader understands the significance. This develops meaning through reference.


Chapter 10:
- Personification: "Dame Nature gathers round a foreign body an envelope of some insensitive tissue which can protect from evil that which it would otherwise harm by contact." Nature is personified as a woman who protects.

- Imagery: "Her breathing was painful to see or hear." This imagery helps the reader visualize Lucy's weak condition.

- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing says "We must obey, and silence is a part of obedience" hinting that something ominous is coming.

- Symbolism: The wreath of garlic that Van Helsing hangs around Lucy's neck symbolizes trying to ward off evil forces.

- Metaphor: "You tell not your madmen what you do nor why you do it; you tell them not what you think." Comparing regular people to "madmen" is a metaphor.


Chapter 11:
- Foreshadowing: Lucy writes that she feels like she is "dying of weakness" and may not survive the night. This foreshadows her death later in the story.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the "myriad of little specks" blowing in through the broken window and "wheeling and circling round like the pillar of dust that travellers describe when there is a simoon in the desert" creates vivid imagery.

- Symbolism: The wreath of flowers given to Lucy by Van Helsing represents protection, so when Lucy's mother tears it off, it symbolizes Lucy losing that protection.

- Personification: The wolf's head appearing in the window is described as if it has human qualities.


Chapter 12:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "It is not yet too late! Quick! quick! Bring the brandy!" it foreshadows that they will try to revive Lucy.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "her throat was bare, showing the two little wounds which we had noticed before, but looking horribly white and mangled" create vivid images for the reader.

- Symbolism: The garlic flowers represent efforts to ward off evil, specifically vampires. When Lucy tears off the garlic flowers, it symbolizes her succumbing to evil.

- Personification: Describing the "flapping of the wings of the angel of death" personifies death.

- Simile: Comparisons like "as one who listens" and "as if the blood, no longer needed for the working of the heart, had gone to make the harshness of death as little rude as might be" use similes.

- Tone: The ominous, tense tone builds suspense around Lucy's health deteriorating.


Chapter 13:
- Personification: "Loneliness will sit over our roofs with brooding wings." Loneliness is given human attributes of sitting and having wings.

- Imagery: "So I can finish this diary; and God only knows if I shall ever begin another. If I do, or if I even open this again, it will be to deal with different people and different themes; for here at the end, where the romance of my life is told, ere I go back to take up the thread of my life-work, I say sadly and without hope, 'FINIS.'" Vivid descriptions of finishing the diary create a visual image.

- Foreshadowing: "Some day he may ask me about it. Down it all goes. I am rusty in my shorthand—see what unexpected prosperity does for us—so it may be as well to freshen it up again with an exercise anyhow...." This foreshadows that Jonathan may later ask about his episode abroad.

- Symbolism: The bloofer lady symbolizes the threat of vampires preying on children.


Chapter 14:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "But, now that I know, I am not afraid, even of the Count. He has succeeded after all, then, in his design in getting to London, and it was he I saw." This foreshadows that Dracula has arrived in London.

- Imagery: Descriptions of Van Helsing like "The poise of the head strikes one at once as indicative of thought and power; the head is noble, well-sized, broad, and large behind the ears." This creates a vivid image of Van Helsing's appearance.

- Symbolism: The repeated mention of blood, like Lucy having "four men's blood in her poor veins" symbolizes life force and the threat to it posed by Dracula.

- Metaphor: Van Helsing says "You are one of the lights. You will have happy life and good life, and your husband will be blessed in you.” Comparing Mina to a light suggests goodness and hope.


Chapter 15:
- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing says "If it be so, farewell" in his note, foreshadowing the danger and difficulty of the task ahead. 

- Imagery: Descriptions of the tomb, coffin, and Lucy's body create vivid images in the reader's mind. For example, "the whites turning to rust and their greens to browns".

- Symbolism: The crucifix and garlic Van Helsing will use to seal Lucy's tomb symbolize tools against evil.

- Personification: "jealousy" is described as being "excepted from the category" of emotions a man would feel, giving the emotion human attributes.

- Allusion: Reference to Lord Byron's poem alludes to literary history.


Chapter 16:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "Two nights hence you shall meet with me and dine together at seven of the clock with friend John. I shall entreat two others, two that you know not as yet; and I shall be ready to all our work show and our plans unfold." This foreshadows that Van Helsing is planning something important involving other characters that will be revealed soon.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the gladness and sorrow of a man’s life" and "the clouds race by, and the passing gleams of the moonlight between the scudding clouds crossing and passing" create vivid imagery.

- Symbolism: The stake and crucifix used against Lucy symbolize the fight against evil.

- Personification: "Her eyes ranged over us" gives human attributes to Lucy's eyes.


Chapter 17:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan says "if ever the future should bring to you a time when you need a man's help, believe me, you will not call in vain", this foreshadows that Mina will likely need Jonathan's help with something in the future. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the tears rained down his cheeks" and "his sorrow was so fresh" create vivid images related to Lord Godalming's grief.

- Personification: The line "that makes a man free to break down before her" personifies "woman's nature" as enabling emotional expression.

- Simile: The description "cried like a wearied child" compares Lord Godalming's sobbing to that of a tired child. 

- Symbolism: Lucy represents goodness and light, as seen when Lord Godalming says Mina loved "poor dear Lucy" and Mina says she and Lucy "were like sisters".


Chapter 18:
- Personification: "When we had finished, Mrs. Harker said:— 'Dr. Seward, may I ask a favour? I want to see your patient, Mr. Renfield.'" This personifies Renfield by referring to him as a "patient" rather than just a man or character.

- Imagery: "When I went into the room, I told the man that a lady would like to see him; to which he simply answered: 'Why?'" This creates a visual image of the scene.

- Foreshadowing: "I thought that the longer this went on the wilder he would get, and so would bring on a fit; so I took him by the hand and raised him up." This foreshadows that Renfield will have a fit.

- Simile: "He suddenly stopped and looked at me intently for several moments. Then, without a word, he rose and moving over, sat down on the side of the bed. The collapse had come, as on former occasion, just as I had expected." Compares Renfield's collapse to previous occasions.

- Symbolism: The crucifix Van Helsing places on the table likely symbolizes their united stand against evil forces.


Chapter 19:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes his dream of seeing Count Dracula's face in the mist, this foreshadows Dracula's later appearances to Jonathan and the others. 

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery throughout to describe the sights, sounds, and smells, such as when describing the foul odor in Dracula's chapel. This helps create an immersive atmosphere.

- Symbolism: The frequent references to blood, like the "acrid smell of blood" in the chapel, symbolize Dracula's vampirism.

- Personification: Descriptions of the mist creeping towards the house "with a sentience and a vitality of its own" personify the mist.


Chapter 20:
- Foreshadowing: The line "This morning the man on duty reported to me that a little after midnight he was restless and kept saying his prayers somewhat loudly." foreshadows that something ominous is about to happen with Renfield.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the windows were encrusted with dust, and the shutters were up. All the framework was black with time" create vivid imagery of the setting.

- Symbolism: The flies that Renfield catches represent his obsession with consuming life forms.

- Metaphor: Calling the purchase money "notes 'over the counter'" is a metaphor for cash payment.

- Personification: The description "the flies, lethargic with the autumn, were beginning to buzz into the room" personifies the flies as lethargic.


Chapter 21:
- Personification: "His eyes flamed red with devilish passion". This describes Dracula's eyes as if they have human emotions and intents.

- Imagery: "The moonlight was so bright that through the thick yellow blind the room was light enough to see". This uses vivid descriptive language to help the reader visualize the scene.

- Foreshadowing: "We must work to-morrow!’ He said the latter words through his shut teeth". This hints that something important is going to happen the next day. 

- Simile: "With a wrench, which threw his victim back upon the bed as though hurled from a height, he turned and sprang at us". This compares Dracula throwing Mina to something being hurled from a height.

- Metaphor: "Then he began promising me things—not in words but by doing them”. This describes Dracula's promises as actions rather than spoken words.

- Symbolism: The blood and vampirism symbolize evil and corruption. Dracula's corruption spreads like a disease.


Chapter 22:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing touches the Sacred Wafer to Mina's forehead and it burns her, marking her with a scar, this foreshadows that she may have to bear this mark until Judgment Day, hinting at her potential transformation into a vampire. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "her overwrought nature had its voice in that dreadful scream" and "Pulling her beautiful hair over her face, as the leper of old his mantle, she wailed out" create vivid images in the reader's mind.

- Symbolism: The scar on Mina's forehead symbolizes that she has been tainted by evil.


Chapter 23:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "He can live for centuries, and you are but mortal woman. Time is now to be dreaded—since once he put that mark upon your throat." This foreshadows that Mina is in grave danger from Dracula because of his immortality. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the red scar on her forehead, of which she was conscious, and which we saw with grinding of our teeth—remembering whence and how it came" create vivid imagery for the reader.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and wafer represent spiritual weapons against Dracula's evil.

- Personification: "Faith is our only anchor" personifies faith as an anchor holding them steady.

- Simile: "We are all drifting reefwards now, and faith is our only anchor" compares their situation to a boat adrift headed towards danger, with faith the only thing keeping them from wrecking.


Chapter 24:
- Personification: "This battle is but begun, and in the end we shall win—so sure as that God sits on high to watch over His children." 

This personifies God as sitting on high and watching over people like a parent watches over children.

- Foreshadowing: "There must be no chance lost, and in Varna we must be ready to act the instant when that ship arrives.” 

This foreshadows that an important event will happen when the ship arrives in Varna.

- Imagery: "The same power that compels her silence may compel her speech."

This creates a vivid image of the supernatural power affecting Mina.

- Symbolism: "We shall all be armed, in all ways; and when the time for the end has come, our effort shall not be lack." 

Being armed symbolizes being prepared to fight the evil Count Dracula.


Chapter 25:
- Personification: "My soul is freer than it has been since that awful hour." The soul is described as if it is a person that can be free or trapped.

- Imagery: "We shall follow him; and we shall not flinch; even if we peril ourselves that we become like him." This creates a vivid image of determinedly pursuing the Count.

- Foreshadowing: "This criminal of ours is predestinate to crime also." This foreshadows that the Count will continue his criminal behavior. 

- Simile: "Just as he fled back over the Danube, leaving his forces to be cut to pieces, so now he is intent on being safe." Compares the Count fleeing from battle to fleeing from the characters.

- Hyperbole: "Give me a fulcrum, and I shall move the world!" Exaggerates Archimedes' quote to emphasize the power of having a starting point.


Chapter 26:
- Foreshadowing: "If the Count escape us this time—and he is strong and subtle and cunning—he may choose to sleep him for a century, and then in time our dear one would come to him to keep him company." This foreshadows the possibility that if they don't stop Dracula now, he may return in the future to turn Mina into a vampire.

- Imagery: "There seem to be endless streams running down the mountains into this river, but as none of them are very large—at present, at all events, though they are terrible doubtless in winter and when the snow melts—the horsemen may not have met much obstruction." This imagery helps the reader visualize the landscape.

- Symbolism: The box carrying Dracula is symbolic of Dracula's confinement and the mystery surrounding his plans.


Chapter 27:
- Foreshadowing: "I began to fear—horrible fears; but then came to me the sense of safety in that ring wherein I stood." This foreshadows the later scene where Van Helsing uses the ring of Host wafers to protect Mina from the vampire women.

- Imagery: "The snow was now falling more heavily, and swirled about fiercely, for a high wind was beginning to blow." This creates a vivid image of the snowstorm. 

- Symbolism: The box carried by the gypsies represents Dracula himself, as it contains his earthly remains. When the box is opened, Dracula is destroyed.

- Personification: "Instinctively they reined in, and at the instant Lord Godalming and Jonathan dashed up at one side and Dr. Seward and Mr. Morris on the other." The horses are described as acting instinctively and reinning themselves in, giving them human-like traits.

- Simile: "It was like a miracle; but before our very eyes, and almost in the drawing of a breath, the whole body crumbled into dust and passed from our sight." Compares Dracula's disintegration to a miracle.
