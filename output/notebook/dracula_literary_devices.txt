
Chapter 1:
- Foreshadowing: The howling of the dogs and wolves foreshadows impending danger and builds suspense. The driver's mysterious behavior also foreshadows sinister events to come.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions of the scenery create strong visual imagery, like "mighty slopes of forest up to the lofty steeps of the Carpathians themselves. Right and left of us they towered, with the afternoon sun falling full upon them."

- Symbolism: The crucifix given to the narrator symbolizes protection against evil forces. The moonlight and dark castle symbolize mystery and foreboding.


Chapter 2:
- Foreshadowing: The descriptions of Dracula's home being like a prison foreshadow that Jonathan will be trapped there.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "a stone falling from the window would fall a thousand feet without touching anything!" create strong mental images.

- Symbolism: The crucifix represents faith which protects Jonathan from Dracula's fury.

- Personification: Describing the dawn as having a "chill" gives human attributes to the non-human dawn.

- Hyperbole: Saying there are doors "everywhere" exaggerates the number for effect.


Chapter 3:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes seeing Dracula crawling down the castle wall, this foreshadows Dracula's supernatural abilities and hints at the horrors to come. 

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe the castle setting, like "great jagged mountain fastnesses, rising peak on peak, the sheer rock studded with mountain ash and thorn." This helps create an ominous, gothic atmosphere.

- Symbolism: The crucifix Jonathan places above his bed symbolizes the struggle between good and evil forces. 

- Metaphor: Dracula's face is described as "deathly pale, and the lines of it were hard like drawn wires" - this metaphor makes him seem dangerous and threatening.

- Personification: The women's laughter is described as "mirthless, hard, soulless" which personifies the sound and highlights its eerie, inhuman quality.


Chapter 4:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan says "I know now the span of my life. God help me!" This foreshadows that Dracula plans to kill him soon.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the women waiting to "suck my blood" creates a vivid image.

- Symbolism: The gold coins symbolize Dracula's greed and wealth.

- Personification: Describing despair as having "its own calms" personifies despair as a person.

- Metaphor: Comparing Dracula to a "filthy leech" metaphorically suggests he is a parasite.

- Simile: Saying the women's laugh "ran away" like water similes their laugh to a flowing liquid.


Chapter 5:
- Personification: "I feel sorry, really and truly sorry, for two of the poor fellows." Lucy personifies the proposals as "poor fellows".

- Imagery: "He put it very nicely, saying that he did not want to wring my confidence from me, but only to know, because if a woman’s heart was free a man might have hope." This creates a vivid image of wringing or squeezing confidence out of Lucy.

- Hyperbole: "I know I would if I were free—only I don’t want to be free. My dear, this quite upset me, and I feel I cannot write of happiness just at once, after telling you of it". Lucy exaggerates her emotions.

- Foreshadowing: "I must only try in the future to show that I am not ungrateful to God for all His goodness to me in sending to me such a lover, such a husband, and such a friend." This foreshadows Lucy's fate.

- Symbolism: The three marriage proposals symbolize Lucy's desirability as a wife, but foreshadow that she will not have a happy marriage.


Chapter 6:
- Foreshadowing: The descriptions of the storm brewing and the strange ship foreshadow ominous events to come later in the story.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions of the sights, sounds, and smells of the seaside town create strong imagery, like "the horizon is lost in a grey mist" and "the sea is tumbling in over the shallows and the sandy flats with a roar, muffled in the sea-mists drifting inland."

- Symbolism: The white lady ghost seen in the abbey ruins symbolizes a foreboding presence. The abbey itself symbolizes the ominous past.

- Personification: The sea and wind are personified, like "the sea is tumbling in" and "there is a 'brool' over the sea that sounds like some presage of doom."


Chapter 7:
- Foreshadowing: The description of the large, strange dog foreshadows its later appearance on the ship with the dead captain. This builds suspense and mystery.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "the sea, running mountains high, threw skywards with each wave mighty masses of white foam" create strong visual imagery. This helps the reader visualize the storm.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and rosary beads tied to the ship captain symbolize his religious devotion. This reveals something about his character.

- Personification: Describing the waves as "a roaring and devouring monster" personifies the sea as a threatening, living thing. This dramatizes the power of the storm.

- Allusion: Referencing Robin Hood when mentioning a local destination alludes to the legendary outlaw. This helps establish the English setting.


Chapter 8:
- Foreshadowing: "I must be careful not to let it grow into a habit. No, I shall take none to-night! I have thought of Lucy, and I shall not dishonour her by mixing the two." This foreshadows Dr. Seward's later addiction to chloral hydrate.

- Imagery: "The setting sun, low down in the sky, was just dropping behind Kettleness; the red light was thrown over on the East Cliff and the old abbey, and seemed to bathe everything in a beautiful rosy glow." This vividly describes the scene using visual imagery.

- Symbolism: The repeated mentions of red eyes symbolize evil and danger. 

- Personification: "Chasing an errant swarm of bees is nothing to following a naked lunatic, when the fit of escaping is upon him!" Personifies the "fit of escaping" as if it has agency.

- Allusion: References to Jack Sheppard allude to the notorious 18th century English robber and escape artist.


Chapter 9:
- Foreshadowing: Mina's letter foreshadows later events when she says "I know you will be anxious to hear all that has happened since we parted at the railway station at Whitby." This hints that important events will unfold.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the marvellous tints that come on foul clouds even as on foul water" use vivid imagery to help the reader visualize the sunset.

- Symbolism: The flies that Renfield catches represent his madness and obsession. When he discards them, it symbolizes him temporarily regaining sanity.

- Dramatic irony: The reader knows Lucy is being targeted by Dracula, but the characters don't, creating irony and tension. 

- Motif: The motif of sunset keeps reappearing, seeming to trigger Renfield's fits and personality changes.


Chapter 10:
- Personification: "Dame Nature gathers round a foreign body an envelope of some insensitive tissue which can protect from evil that which it would otherwise harm by contact." This personifies nature as actively protecting Lucy's body.

- Imagery: Descriptions of Lucy like "horribly white and wan-looking" and "ghastly, chalkily pale" create vivid images of her worsening condition.

- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing rubbing garlic on the windows and door foreshadows upcoming supernatural events involving vampires.

- Simile: "Her breathing was stronger; I could see the counterpane move as her breast heaved. By the bedside sat Van Helsing, looking at her intently. The velvet band again covered the red mark. I asked the Professor in a whisper:— “What do you make of that mark on her throat?” “What do you make of it?” “I have not examined it yet,” I answered, and then and there proceeded to loose the band. Just over the external jugular vein there were two punctures, not large, but not wholesome-looking. There was no sign of disease, but the edges were white and worn-looking, as if by some trituration. It at once occurred to me that this wound, or whatever it was, might be the means of that manifest loss of blood; but I abandoned the idea as soon as formed, for such a thing could not be. The whole bed would have been drenched to a scarlet with the blood which the girl must have lost to leave such a pallor as she had before the transfusion." This simile compares the puncture wounds on Lucy's neck to the cause of her blood loss.


Chapter 11:
- Foreshadowing: Lucy writes "I feel I am dying of weakness, and have barely strength to write, but it must be done if I die in the doing." This foreshadows her later death.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the pillar of dust that travellers describe when there is a simoon in the desert" create vivid imagery.

- Symbolism: The wreath of flowers that Lucy tears off symbolizes Dr. Van Helsing's protections being removed.

- Personification: "Somewhere near, a passing bell was tolling; the dogs all round the neighbourhood were howling; and in our shrubbery, seemingly just outside, a nightingale was singing." The passing bell, dogs, and nightingale are personified as mourning Lucy's death.


Chapter 12:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "It is not yet too late! Quick! quick! Bring the brandy!" This foreshadows that they will try to revive Lucy.

- Imagery: Descriptions of Lucy like "her throat was bare, showing the two little wounds which we had noticed before, but looking horribly white and mangled." This imagery shows Lucy's worsening condition.

- Symbolism: The garlic flowers represent trying to ward off evil, specifically vampirism. When Lucy tears off the flowers, it symbolizes her succumbing to vampirism.

- Personification: "the flapping of the wings of the angel of death" personifies death.

- Allusion: Reference to the poem "We thought her dying whilst she slept, And sleeping when she died." alludes to Lucy's death.


Chapter 13:
- Personification: "Loneliness will sit over our roofs with brooding wings." This gives loneliness human-like qualities.

- Imagery: "Where the air is fresh, and the sun rises over Hampstead Hill, and where wild flowers grow of their own accord." This creates a vivid image in the reader's mind.  

- Foreshadowing: "There will be pain for us all; but it will not be all pain, nor will this pain be the last." This hints at future events.

- Simile: "Some of our caricaturists might, he says, take a lesson in the irony of grotesque by comparing the reality and the picture." This directly compares two things using "like" or "as."

- Symbolism: The "bloofer lady" seems to symbolize death/danger for the children.


Chapter 14:
- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing says "There are darknesses in life, and there are lights; you are one of the lights. You will have happy life and good life, and your husband will be blessed in you." This foreshadows that dark events will happen later in the story.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "his eyebrows came down and the mouth tightened" and "noble, well-sized, broad, and large behind the ears" create vivid images in the reader's mind.

- Symbolism: Lucy's mysterious illness and death symbolize the unknown supernatural threat.

- Dramatic irony: The reader knows Lucy was bitten by Dracula, but the characters do not yet realize this. 

- Personification: "That faculty which enables us to believe things which we know to be untrue" personifies faith.


Chapter 15:
- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing says "If it be so, farewell" in his note, foreshadowing the dangerous events that will unfold at the cemetery.

- Imagery: Descriptions of Lucy's appearance in the tomb, the dreary cemetery setting, and Van Helsing's actions create vivid images.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and garlic Van Helsing uses represent tools against evil forces.

- Personification: "Age by age they may solve only in part" personifies the ages working to solve mysteries.  

- Metaphor: "He had always the strength in his hand of twenty men" metaphorically describes the vampire's power.


Chapter 16:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "Two nights hence you shall meet with me and dine together at seven of the clock with friend John. I shall entreat two others, two that you know not as yet; and I shall be ready to all our work show and our plans unfold." This foreshadows that Van Helsing is planning to meet with the group in two nights to reveal more of his plans for defeating Dracula.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the gladness and sorrow of a man’s life" and "the clouds race by, and the passing gleams of the moonlight between the scudding clouds crossing and passing" use vivid imagery to convey the scene.

- Symbolism: The stake and crucifix are symbolic of the tools used to defeat the evil of the vampires.


Chapter 17:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan says "God grant that no such time may ever come to you to break the sunshine of your life; but if it should ever come, promise me that you will let me know", this foreshadows that Mina will likely need Jonathan's help in the future as they battle Dracula. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the tears rained down his cheeks" and "he grew quite hysterical, and raising his open hands, beat his palms together in a perfect agony of grief" create vivid images of Lord Godalming's intense grief.

- Symbolism: The kiss Mina gives Morris symbolizes comfort and friendship. 

- Personification: The line "if ever the future should bring to you a time when you need a man’s help" personifies the future as an entity that can actively bring things to people.


Chapter 18:
- Personification: "When we had finished, Mrs. Harker said:— 'Dr. Seward, may I ask a favour? I want to see your patient, Mr. Renfield. Do let me see him. What you have said of him in your diary interests me so much!'" Here, Mrs. Harker is personifying Dr. Seward's diary by saying it "interests" her. The diary is given human qualities of having interests.

- Imagery: "He can transform himself to wolf, as we gather from the ship arrival in Whitby, when he tear open the dog; he can be as bat, as Madam Mina saw him on the window at Whitby, and as friend John saw him fly from this so near house, and as my friend Quincey saw him at the window of Miss Lucy." This passage uses vivid imagery to describe Dracula's ability to transform into different creatures. 

- Foreshadowing: "Take me with you, friend John,” said the Professor. “His case in your diary interest me much, and it had bearing, too, now and again on our case. I should much like to see him, and especial when his mind is disturbed.” Van Helsing's interest in Renfield foreshadows Renfield's importance in relation to Dracula.

- Symbolism: "The Draculas were, says Arminius, a great and noble race, though now and again were scions who were held by their coevals to have had dealings with the Evil One. They learned his secrets in the Scholomance, amongst the mountains over Lake Hermanstadt, where the devil claims the tenth scholar as his due." The name Dracula and his association with the devil symbolizes the evil he represents.

Chapter 19:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes his disturbing dream of seeing Count Dracula's face in the mist, this foreshadows Dracula's later appearances to Jonathan and the danger he poses. 

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery throughout to describe the sights, sounds, and smells, such as when describing the foul odor in Dracula's chapel or the swarming rats. This helps create an immersive, creepy atmosphere.

- Symbolism: The frequent references to blood, like the "acrid smell of blood" in Dracula's chapel, symbolize the life force Dracula takes from his victims. Blood is a recurring symbol of both life and death.

- Personification: When the thin streak of mist seems to have a "sentience and vitality of its own," Stoker uses personification to make the inanimate mist seem alive and sinister.

- Metaphor: Dracula's castle is described as a "chess game" that Van Helsing and the others must strategize to defeat Dracula, the king piece. This extended metaphor emphasizes the idea of an unfolding battle of wits.


Chapter 20:
- Foreshadowing: "If we could only get some hint as to what passed in his mind, between the time of my argument with him to-day and his resumption of fly-catching, it might afford us a valuable clue." This foreshadows that something important is going to happen with Renfield.

- Imagery: "His brows contracted, and he stood up and walked about the room." This creates a visual image of Lord Godalming's reaction.

- Personification: "The flies, lethargic with the autumn, were beginning to buzz into the room." The flies are described as if they have human traits.

- Simile: "He smiled with an ineffably benign superiority." Compares his smile to something using "like" or "as."

- Symbolism: The flies symbolize death and decay. Renfield's obsession with flies symbolizes his dark desires.


Chapter 21:
- Personification: "His eyes flamed red with devilish passion". This describes Dracula's eyes as if they have human emotions.

- Imagery: "The moonlight was so bright that through the thick yellow blind the room was light enough to see". This creates a vivid image in the reader's mind of the bright moonlight illuminating the room.

- Foreshadowing: "We must work to-morrow!’ He said the latter words through his shut teeth". This foreshadows that something important is going to happen the next day. 

- Simile: "With a wrench, which threw his victim back upon the bed as though hurled from a height, he turned and sprang at us". This compares Dracula throwing Mina back on the bed to her being hurled from a height.

- Metaphor: "Then indeed, my heart sank within me: beside the bed, as if he had stepped out of the mist—or rather as if the mist had turned into his figure, for it had entirely disappeared—stood a tall, thin man, all in black". This metaphorically describes Dracula emerging from the mist as if the mist had transformed into his figure.


Chapter 22:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing touches the Sacred Wafer to Mina's forehead and it burns her, marking her with a scar, this foreshadows that she may have to become a vampire. The scar is described as a "mark of shame" that she may have to bear until Judgment Day, foreshadowing her potential transformation.

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe the scar on Mina's forehead, comparing it to a "white-hot metal" searing her flesh. This helps the reader visualize the intense pain and traumatic nature of the moment.

- Symbolism: The Sacred Wafer burning Mina is symbolic, representing the idea that she has been marked by evil in some way. The wafer is meant to provide protection, so its burning her instead symbolizes that she is now vulnerable to the dark forces they are fighting.

- Metaphor: When Mina covers her face with her hair like a "leper of old his mantle," this metaphorically conveys her feelings of being unclean and contaminated by the scar on her forehead.

- Personification: The line "That monster must retain whatever form he now has" personifies Count Dracula as a monster. This conveys the sense of evil and danger he represents.


Chapter 23:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "He can live for centuries, and you are but mortal woman. Time is now to be dreaded—since once he put that mark upon your throat." This foreshadows that Mina is in grave danger from Dracula because of his immortality. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the red streak shot up, and a rosy light seemed to diffuse itself through the room" and "the sound of men stamping overhead as they run about" create vivid images for the reader.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and wafer represent spiritual weapons against Dracula's evil.

- Personification: "Sleep has no place it can call its own" personifies sleep.

- Simile: "We can know now what was in the Count’s mind, when he seize that money, though Jonathan’s so fierce knife put him in the danger that even he dread. He meant escape. Hear me, ESCAPE! He saw that with but one earth-box left, and a pack of men following like dogs after a fox, this London was no place for him." Compares the men pursuing Dracula to dogs chasing a fox.


Chapter 24:
- Personification: "This battle is but begun, and in the end we shall win—so sure as that God sits on high to watch over His children." 

This personifies God as sitting on high and watching over people like a parent watches over children.

- Foreshadowing: "There must be no chance lost, and in Varna we must be ready to act the instant when that ship arrives.” 

This foreshadows that an important event will happen when the ship arrives in Varna.

- Imagery: "The same power that compels her silence may compel her speech."

This creates a vivid image of the powerful hold the vampire has over Mina.

- Simile: “Quincey’s head is level at all times, but most so when there is to hunt, metaphor be more dishonour to science than wolves be of danger to man.”

This compares Quincey's steady thinking to the danger of wolves, using "as" and "than" to draw the simile.

- Symbolism: “We shall all be armed, in all ways; and when the time for the end has come, our effort shall not be lack.” 

The weapons symbolize their preparation and resolve to fight the vampire.


Chapter 25:
- Personification: "My soul is freer than it has been since that awful hour." The soul is described as if it is a person that can be free or trapped.

- Foreshadowing: "He think, too, that as he cut himself off from knowing your mind, there can be no knowledge of him to you; there is where he fail!" This foreshadows that Dracula's belief he has escaped them will prove to be wrong.

- Imagery: "We shall follow him; and we shall not flinch; even if we peril ourselves that we become like him." This evokes a vivid image of the characters relentlessly pursuing Dracula no matter the risk.

- Symbolism: Dracula fleeing back to Transylvania symbolizes his retreat when defeated, before regrouping and trying again.

- Metaphor: "That terrible baptism of blood which he give you makes you free to go to him in spirit." Mina's forced blood exchange with Dracula is compared to a baptism that gives her a metaphysical connection to him.


Chapter 26:
- Foreshadowing: "If the Count escape us this time—and he is strong and subtle and cunning—he may choose to sleep him for a century, and then in time our dear one would come to him to keep him company." This foreshadows the possibility that if they don't stop Dracula now, he may return in the future to turn Mina into a vampire.

- Imagery: "With every boat which we have over-hauled since then this trick has succeeded; we have had every deference shown to us, and not once any objection to whatever we chose to ask or do. Some of the Slovaks tell us that a big boat passed them, going at more than usual speed as she had a double crew on board." This imagery helps the reader visualize the scene and actions.

- Symbolism: The box carrying Dracula is symbolic of the evil he represents. "Hildesheim cleared the box, took it off, and gave it to Skinsky. Skinsky took it—and here we lose the trail." The box's movement symbolizes Dracula's unknown plans.


Chapter 27:
- Foreshadowing: "I began to fear—horrible fears; but then came to me the sense of safety in that ring wherein I stood." This foreshadows the later scene where Van Helsing uses the ring of Host wafers to protect Mina from the vampire women.

- Imagery: "The snow was now falling more heavily, and swirled about fiercely, for a high wind was beginning to blow." This creates a vivid image of the snowstorm. 

- Symbolism: The box carried by the gypsies symbolizes Dracula's earthly remains. 

- Personification: "Instinctively they reined in, and at the instant Lord Godalming and Jonathan dashed up at one side and Dr. Seward and Mr. Morris on the other." The horses are described as acting instinctively and reinning themselves in, giving them human-like qualities.

- Metaphor: "The dying man spoke:— 'Now God be thanked that all has not been in vain! See! the snow is not more stainless than her forehead! The curse has passed away!'" This metaphorically compares Mina's pure forehead to the white snow.
