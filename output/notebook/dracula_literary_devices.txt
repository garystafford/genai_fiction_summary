
Chapter 1:
- Foreshadowing: The howling of the dogs and wolves foreshadows the sense of danger and supernatural events to come later in the passage.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the sights and sounds of the journey evoke vivid images, like "the steam from our hard-driven horses rose in a white cloud."

- Symbolism: The crucifix given to the narrator symbolizes the fear of evil forces and things unknown.


Chapter 2:
- Foreshadowing: The descriptions of Dracula's home being like a prison foreshadow that Jonathan will be trapped there.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "a stone falling from the window would fall a thousand feet without touching anything!" create strong mental images.

- Symbolism: The crucifix represents faith which protects Jonathan from Dracula's fury.

- Personification: Describing the dawn as having a "chill" gives human attributes to the non-human dawn.

- Metaphor: Comparing Dracula's teeth to sharp white fangs uses metaphor to emphasize his predatory nature.


Chapter 3:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes seeing Dracula crawling down the castle wall, it foreshadows Dracula's supernatural abilities and hints at the dangers to come. This builds suspense and tension.

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe the setting and events, like "great jagged mountain fastnesses, rising peak on peak, the sheer rock studded with mountain ash and thorn." This helps the reader visualize the remote Transylvanian landscape.

- Symbolism: The crucifix Jonathan touches represents his Christian faith and provides comfort, symbolizing the power of his beliefs against Dracula's evil.

- Metaphor: Dracula's speech comparing his ancestors to lions fighting for lordship uses metaphor to emphasize their fierceness and warrior spirit.

- Tone: The ominous, uneasy tone builds an atmosphere of mystery and dread surrounding Dracula and the castle.


Chapter 4:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan says "I know now the span of my life. God help me!" This foreshadows that Dracula plans to kill him soon.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the women waiting to "suck my blood" creates a vivid image.

- Symbolism: The gold coins symbolize Dracula's greed and wealth.

- Personification: Describing despair as having "its own calms" personifies despair as a person.

- Metaphor: Comparing Dracula to a "filthy leech" creates a metaphor between Dracula's blood-drinking and a leech's blood-sucking.

- Simile: When Jonathan says the women's laugh was "like a horrible laugh" it makes a simile comparing the laugh to something horrible.


Chapter 5:
- Personification: "I feel sorry, really and truly sorry, for two of the poor fellows." Lucy personifies the proposals as "poor fellows".

- Hyperbole: "Some girls are so vain! You and I, Mina dear, who are engaged and are going to settle down soon soberly into old married women, can despise vanity." Lucy exaggerates that she and Mina will soon be "old married women". 

- Imagery: "He put it very nicely, saying that he did not want to wring my confidence from me, but only to know, because if a woman’s heart was free a man might have hope." Lucy describes Dr. Seward's proposal using visual imagery.

- Foreshadowing: "I know now what I would do if I were a man and wanted to make a girl love me." Lucy foreshadows her love for Arthur.

- Symbolism: "I burst into tears—I am afraid, my dear, you will think this a very sloppy letter in more ways than one—and I really felt very badly." Lucy's tears symbolize her anguish over having to reject Morris' proposal.


Chapter 6:
- Foreshadowing: The descriptions of the storm brewing and the strange ship foreshadow ominous events to come.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions of the sights, sounds, and smells of the seaside town create strong imagery. For example, "the horizon is lost in a grey mist" and "the sea is tumbling in over the shallows and the sandy flats with a roar, muffled in the sea-mists drifting inland."

- Personification: The sea and wind are described with human attributes, like "the sea that sounds like some presage of doom" and "there’s something in that wind and in the hoast beyont that sounds, and looks, and tastes, and smells like death."


Chapter 7:
- Foreshadowing: The description of the large, strange dog foreshadows later events when it mysteriously appears on the ship. The author builds suspense and hints that the dog will be important later in the story.

- Imagery: Vivid descriptions like "the sea, running mountains high, threw skywards with each wave mighty masses of white foam" create strong visual imagery for the reader.

- Symbolism: The crucifix tied to the dead captain's hands symbolizes his faith and commitment to his duty.

- Personification: The sea is personified as a "roaring and devouring monster" which helps the reader visualize the intensity of the storm.

- Allusion: References to Robin Hood's Bay and the North Foreland allude to real places, helping to ground the story in reality.


Chapter 8:
- Foreshadowing: "I must be careful not to let it grow into a habit. No, I shall take none to-night! I have thought of Lucy, and I shall not dishonour her by mixing the two." This foreshadows Dr. Seward's later addiction to chloral hydrate.

- Imagery: "The setting sun, low down in the sky, was just dropping behind Kettleness; the red light was thrown over on the East Cliff and the old abbey, and seemed to bathe everything in a beautiful rosy glow." This vividly describes the scene using visual imagery. 

- Symbolism: The repeated mentions of red eyes symbolize evil and danger. 

- Personification: "Chasing an errant swarm of bees is nothing to following a naked lunatic, when the fit of escaping is upon him!" The fit of escaping is personified as a living thing upon the lunatic.

- Allusion: References to Jack Sheppard allude to the notorious 18th century English robber and escape artist.


Chapter 9:
- Foreshadowing: Mina's letter foreshadows later events when she says "I know you will be anxious to hear all that has happened since we parted at the railway station at Whitby." This hints that important events will unfold.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the marvellous tints that come on foul clouds even as on foul water" create vivid imagery.

- Symbolism: The sunset Seward describes while Lucy is yelling likely symbolizes Lucy's life fading away.

- Personification: Referring to the "smoky beauty" of the sunset personifies it.

- Allusion: Mention of Disraeli alludes to the famous British prime minister.


Chapter 10:
- Personification: "Dame Nature gathers round a foreign body an envelope of some insensitive tissue which can protect from evil that which it would otherwise harm by contact." This personifies nature as actively protecting something.

- Imagery: "Her lips were slightly parted, and her breast rose and fell with the regularity of a pendulum." This creates a vivid visual image of Lucy's breathing.

- Foreshadowing: The description of Lucy's mysterious puncture wounds on her neck and blood loss foreshadows later supernatural events.

- Symbolism: The wreath of garlic flowers symbolizes Van Helsing's efforts to protect Lucy from evil forces. 

- Dramatic irony: The characters don't realize the supernatural cause of Lucy's condition, but the reader does based on foreshadowing.


Chapter 11:
- Foreshadowing: Lucy writes that she feels like she is "dying of weakness" and may not survive the night. This foreshadows her death later in the chapter.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the "myriad of little specks" blowing in through the broken window and "wheeling and circling round like the pillar of dust" create vivid imagery.

- Symbolism: The wreath of flowers given to Lucy by Van Helsing represents protection, so when Lucy's mother tears it off, it symbolizes Lucy losing that protection.

- Personification: The wind is personified when the "window blind blew back with the wind that rushed in."


Chapter 12:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "It is not yet too late! Quick! quick! Bring the brandy!" it foreshadows that they will try to revive Lucy.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "her throat was bare, showing the two little wounds which we had noticed before, but looking horribly white and mangled" create vivid images for the reader.

- Symbolism: The garlic flowers represent efforts to ward off evil, specifically vampires. When Lucy tears off the garlic flowers, it symbolizes her succumbing to evil.

- Personification: Describing the "flapping of the wings of the angel of death" personifies death.

- Simile: "She lay quite still, and I looked round the room to see that all was as it should be. I could see that the Professor had carried out in this room, as in the other, his purpose of using the garlic; the whole of the window-sashes reeked with it, and round Lucy's neck, over the silk handkerchief which Van Helsing made her keep on, was a rough chaplet of the same odorous flowers." Compares the garlic flowers to a chaplet with the simile "a rough chaplet."

- Tone: The ominous, tense tone builds suspense around Lucy's health deteriorating and the threat of vampires.


Chapter 13:
- Personification: "Loneliness will sit over our roofs with brooding wings." This gives loneliness human-like qualities.

- Imagery: "Where the air is fresh, and the sun rises over Hampstead Hill, and where wild flowers grow of their own accord." This creates a vivid image in the reader's mind.  

- Foreshadowing: "There will be pain for us all; but it will not be all pain, nor will this pain be the last." This hints at future events.

- Simile: "Some of our caricaturists might, he says, take a lesson in the irony of grotesque by comparing the reality and the picture." This directly compares two things using "like" or "as."

- Motif: The repeated mentions of the "bloofer lady" ties into the overall motif of the supernatural in the story.


Chapter 14:
- Foreshadowing: Van Helsing says "There are darknesses in life, and there are lights; you are one of the lights. You will have happy life and good life, and your husband will be blessed in you." This foreshadows that dark events will happen later in the story.

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery to describe Van Helsing, like "the poise of the head strikes one at once as indicative of thought and power; the head is noble, well-sized, broad, and large behind the ears." This helps the reader visualize the character.

- Symbolism: Lucy's mysterious illness and puncture wounds on her neck symbolize her transformation into a vampire.

- Dramatic irony: The characters don't realize Lucy was bitten by a vampire bat and is turning into a vampire herself, but the reader knows. 

- Personification: "Never, even in the midst of our despair about poor Lucy, had he looked more stern." Despair is given human attributes.


Chapter 15:
- Foreshadowing: "There lay Lucy, seemingly just as we had seen her the night before her funeral. She was, if possible, more radiantly beautiful than ever." This foreshadows that Lucy has become undead.

- Imagery: "When the spider and the beetle had resumed their accustomed dominance; when time-discoloured stone, and dust-encrusted mortar, and rusty, dank iron, and tarnished brass, and clouded silver-plating gave back the feeble glimmer of a candle, the effect was more miserable and sordid than could have been imagined." This vividly describes the dreary scene in the tomb.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and garlic that Van Helsing uses are symbols of warding off evil.


Chapter 16:
- Imagery: "The night was dark with occasional gleams of moonlight between the rents of the heavy clouds that scudded across the sky." This description uses vivid imagery to help the reader visualize the setting.

- Foreshadowing: "When she saw us she drew back with an angry snarl, such as a cat gives when taken unawares." This foreshadows Lucy's later violent, animalistic behavior. 

- Personification: "There was gladness and mirth and peace everywhere, for we were at rest ourselves on one account." Gladness, mirth, and peace are given human attributes.

- Simile: "He looked like a figure of Thor as his untrembling arm rose and fell." This compares Arthur's strength to the Norse god Thor.

- Metaphor: "You are now in the bitter waters, my child." The difficulties Arthur faces are compared to "bitter waters."


Chapter 17:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan says "God grant that no such time may ever come to you to break the sunshine of your life; but if it should ever come, promise me that you will let me know", this foreshadows that Mina will likely need Jonathan's help against Dracula in the future. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the tears rained down his cheeks" and "his sorrow was so fresh" create vivid images related to Lord Godalming's grief.

- Symbolism: Lucy's death represents the loss of innocence.

- Personification: The line "my heart bled for him" personifies the heart.

- Simile: Mina comforting Lord Godalming is compared to "a wearied child".


Chapter 18:
- Personification: "When we had finished, Mrs. Harker said:— 'Dr. Seward, may I ask a favour? I want to see your patient, Mr. Renfield. Do let me see him. What you have said of him in your diary interests me so much!'" (Mrs. Harker is personified as wanting to see Renfield.)

- Foreshadowing: "When I went to the station to meet Van Helsing I left the boys behind me. Poor Art seemed more cheerful than he has been since Lucy first took ill, and Quincey is more like his own bright self than he has been for many a long day." (This foreshadows coming events related to Lucy.)

- Imagery: "He can transform himself to wolf, as we gather from the ship arrival in Whitby, when he tear open the dog; he can be as bat, as Madam Mina saw him on the window at Whitby, and as friend John saw him fly from this so near house, and as my friend Quincey saw him at the window of Miss Lucy." (Visual imagery of Dracula transforming.)

- Simile: "He shook his head sadly, and with a look of poignant regret on his face." (Comparing Renfield's expression to poignant regret.)


Chapter 19:
- Foreshadowing: When Jonathan describes his disturbing dream of seeing Count Dracula's face in the mist, this foreshadows Dracula's later appearances to Jonathan and the others. 

- Imagery: Stoker uses vivid imagery throughout to describe the sights, sounds, and smells, such as when the men enter the chapel and encounter the overpowering stench. This engages the reader's senses.

- Symbolism: The frequent references to light vs dark and day vs night symbolize the battle between good and evil. Light is associated with the heroes while darkness is associated with Dracula.

- Personification: The white mist creeping towards the house is personified as having "a sentience and a vitality of its own."

- Allusion: When Renfield prays for a cat, this alludes to his earlier manic obsession with consuming living creatures to absorb their life force.


Chapter 20:
- Foreshadowing: The line "We seem at last to be on the track, and our work of to-morrow may be the beginning of the end." foreshadows that the characters may be close to defeating Count Dracula. 

- Imagery: Descriptions like "the shutters were encrusted with dust" and "the windows were encrusted with dust" create vivid images for the reader.

- Symbolism: The boxes of earth symbolize Dracula's plan to spread his influence across London.

- Personification: The line "the flies, lethargic with the autumn, were beginning to buzz into the room" personifies the flies as lethargic.

- Allusion: Referring to Shakespeare's quote "Rats and mice and such small deer" alludes to another famous work of literature.


Chapter 21:
- Personification: "His eyes flamed red with devilish passion". This describes Dracula's eyes as if they have human emotions.

- Imagery: "The moonlight was so bright that through the thick yellow blind the room was light enough to see". This creates a vivid image in the reader's mind of the bright moonlight in the room.

- Foreshadowing: "We must work to-morrow!’ He said the latter words through his shut teeth". This foreshadows that something important is going to happen the next day. 

- Simile: "With a wrench, which threw his victim back upon the bed as though hurled from a height, he turned and sprang at us". This compares Dracula throwing Mina back on the bed to her being hurled from a height.

- Metaphor: "Then indeed, my heart sank within me". This metaphorically describes the feeling of her heart sinking due to fear, instead of literally sinking.

- Symbolism: The blood symbolizes Dracula's evil and darkness. For example, "Her white nightdress was smeared with blood".


Chapter 22:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing puts the Sacred Wafer on Mina's forehead and it burns her, foreshadowing that she may have to bear the mark until Judgment Day. This hints that she may eventually become a vampire.

- Imagery: Descriptions of the creepy, dusty house in Piccadilly create vivid images in the reader's mind and build the gothic atmosphere. 

- Symbolism: The boxes of earth symbolize the Count's corruption spreading.

- Dramatic irony: The characters don't know yet that destroying the boxes won't be enough to stop the Count. The reader knows their efforts will ultimately fail to prevent Mina's fate.


Chapter 23:
- Foreshadowing: When Van Helsing says "He can live for centuries, and you are but mortal woman. Time is now to be dreaded—since once he put that mark upon your throat." This foreshadows that Mina is in grave danger from Dracula and his immortality.

- Imagery: Descriptions like "a drawn, haggard old man, whose white hair matches well with the hollow burning eyes and grief-written lines of his face." Help the reader visualize the characters and scenes.

- Symbolism: The crucifix and wafer represent spiritual weapons against Dracula's evil.

- Personification: "The gladness of our hearts must have shown upon our faces." Gives human attributes to the abstract concept of gladness.


Chapter 24:
- Personification: "This battle is but begun, and in the end we shall win—so sure as that God sits on high to watch over His children." 

This personifies God as sitting on high and watching over people like a parent watches over children.

- Foreshadowing: "There must be no chance lost, and in Varna we must be ready to act the instant when that ship arrives.” 

This foreshadows that an important event will happen when the ship arrives in Varna.

- Imagery: "The same power that compels her silence may compel her speech."

This creates a vivid image of the powerful force influencing Mina.

- Symbolism: "We shall all be armed, in all ways; and when the time for the end has come, our effort shall not be lack."

Being armed symbolizes being prepared to fight the evil forces they face.

- Dramatic irony: "I shall not wait for any opportunity, said Morris. "When I see the box I shall open it and destroy the monster, though there were a thousand men looking on, and if I am to be wiped out for it the next moment!" 

The reader knows Morris' brave words are tragically misguided about what it will take to defeat the monster.


Chapter 25:
- Personification: "My soul is freer than it has been since that awful hour." The soul is described as if it is a person that can be free or trapped.

- Foreshadowing: "He think, too, that as he cut himself off from knowing your mind, there can be no knowledge of him to you; there is where he fail!" This foreshadows that cutting himself off from Mina's mind will actually allow them to find Dracula.

- Imagery: "We shall follow him; and we shall not flinch; even if we peril ourselves that we become like him." This creates a vivid image of the characters relentlessly pursuing Dracula no matter the risk.

- Simile: "But his child-mind only saw so far; and it may be that, as ever is in God’s Providence, the very thing that the evil-doer most reckoned on for his selfish good, turns out to be his chiefest harm." Dracula's limited thinking is compared to a child's.

- Symbolism: Mina's "baptism of blood" symbolizes her connection to Dracula after he fed on her.


Chapter 26:
- Foreshadowing: "If the Count escape us this time—and he is strong and subtle and cunning—he may choose to sleep him for a century, and then in time our dear one would come to him to keep him company." This foreshadows the possibility that if they don't stop Dracula now, he may return in the future to turn Mina into a vampire.

- Imagery: "With every boat which we have over-hauled since then this trick has succeeded; we have had every deference shown to us, and not once any objection to whatever we chose to ask or do. Some of the Slovaks tell us that a big boat passed them, going at more than usual speed as she had a double crew on board." This imagery helps the reader visualize the scene and actions.

- Symbolism: The box carrying Dracula is symbolic of the evil he represents. "Skinsky took it—and here we lose the trail. We only know that the box is somewhere on the water, moving along." The box's unknown whereabouts builds suspense.


Chapter 27:
- Foreshadowing: "I began to fear—horrible fears; but then came to me the sense of safety in that ring wherein I stood." This foreshadows the later scene where Van Helsing draws a circle to protect Mina from the vampire women.

- Imagery: "The snow was now falling more heavily, and swirled about fiercely, for a high wind was beginning to blow." This creates a vivid image of the snowstorm. 

- Symbolism: The box carried by the gypsies represents Dracula's coffin. Its movement symbolizes Dracula getting closer to the characters.

- Metaphor: "The sun was now right down upon the mountain top, and the shadows of the whole group fell long upon the snow." The shadows represent the impending doom closing in on the characters.

- Personification: "Instinctively they reined in, and at the instant Lord Godalming and Jonathan dashed up at one side and Dr. Seward and Mr. Morris on the other." The horses are personified as acting instinctively.
